CHATGPT CHALLENG ACHITECTURE:
## Challenge Architecture

Forge is not a reading tracker.

Forge is not a habit tracker.

Forge is a competitive accountability platform built around challenges.

The architecture must be challenge-first, not activity-first.

Every feature, screen, database model, and user flow should assume that Forge may host many different challenges in the future.

Examples include:

* Exam Flame
* ReadHabit Challenge
* CodeHabit Challenge
* Clash of Halls
* Future competitions that do not yet exist

Because of this, Forge must never be hardcoded around reading, books, study sessions, or exam preparation.

Instead, Forge revolves around a central Challenge entity.

A challenge contains:

* Name
* Description
* Start Date
* End Date
* Duration
* Prize Structure
* Rules
* Status
* Participants
* Leaderboards
* Submissions

Examples:

Exam Flame is a challenge.

ReadHabit is a challenge.

CodeHabit is a challenge.

Users do not belong to a challenge permanently.

Users join challenges.

A single user account should be capable of participating in multiple challenges over time.

Example:

Prince
├── Exam Flame
├── ReadHabit
└── CodeHabit

This architecture ensures Forge scales beyond a single event.

---

### Challenge Lifecycle

Every challenge must move through defined states.

Draft

Challenge is being prepared and is not visible publicly.

Active

Challenge is currently running and accepting submissions.

Verification

Challenge has ended but administrators are reviewing submissions and resolving disputes.

Completed

Results are finalized and rankings are locked.

Archived

Challenge is preserved historically and can no longer be modified.

---

### Submission Architecture

Forge must never store leaderboard totals directly.

The source of truth is daily submissions.

Each participant submits activity for a specific challenge day.

Example:

Day 1 → 8 Hours

Day 2 → 10.5 Hours

Day 3 → 7 Hours

Total hours should always be calculated from submissions.

Leaderboards must be generated dynamically from verified submissions.

This guarantees data integrity and prevents ranking inconsistencies.

---

### Submission Workflow

Each submission contains:

* Hours Completed
* Study Topic
* Screenshot Evidence
* Timestamp
* Verification Status

Optional:

* Notes

Submission states:

Pending

Awaiting review.

Confirmed

Verified and counted toward rankings.

Rejected

Disqualified and excluded from calculations.

No submission should ever be permanently deleted.

All submission history must remain available for auditing.

---

### Leaderboard Architecture

Leaderboards are the core of Forge.

The leaderboard is not a secondary feature.

The leaderboard is the product.

Users primarily open Forge to answer:

* Am I winning?
* Who passed me?
* How far am I from the prize line?
* Who am I need to overtake?
* Who is chasing me?

Because of this, the leaderboard must update automatically as verified submissions change.

For Exam Flame, ranking is determined by:

1. Total Verified Hours
2. Number of Verified Days
3. Earliest Submission Timestamp

The challenge organizer may customize ranking logic in future challenges.

---

### Prize Line System

Forge must support configurable prize positions.

Example:

Top 15

Top 10

Top 5

The system should automatically calculate the prize threshold based on challenge settings.

A visible "Prize Line" must appear within the leaderboard.

Participants below the line should see exactly how many hours separate them from qualification.

Example:

7.5 Hours to Cross

This is a critical motivational feature.

---

### Rivalry System

Forge should create tension.

Every participant dashboard should display:

Current Rank

Person Directly Above You

Hours Needed to Overtake

Person Directly Below You

Hours Lead Before Being Overtaken

Example:

Chasing:
Samuel Idi
+2.5 Hours Ahead

Being Chased By:
David Olubi
-1.0 Hour Behind

The rivalry system should make rankings feel alive and dynamic.

---

### Challenge Progress System

Each challenge day should be visually represented.

For a 20-day challenge:

20 progress cells should be displayed.

Verified Day:
Filled

Pending Day:
Patterned

Skipped Day:
Empty

The progress strip acts as a visual campaign tracker.

Participants should immediately understand how far they have progressed through the challenge.

---

### Hall Architecture

Forge should be architected to support halls as first-class entities.

Users may optionally belong to halls.

Examples:

Nelson Hall

Akpovi Hall

Hall 2

A challenge may enable:

Individual Competition

Hall Competition

Or Both

When enabled, halls should accumulate total verified hours from all members.

This allows future hall-versus-hall challenges without requiring architectural changes.

---

### Admin Architecture

Administrators do not need traditional dashboards.

Forge should prioritize moderation queues.

The admin workflow should be:

Review Submission

Approve or Reject

Move Immediately To Next Submission

The verification process must be optimized for speed and scale.

The system should comfortably support hundreds of submissions without administrative fatigue.

Keyboard shortcuts and rapid review workflows are encouraged.

---

### Long-Term Scalability

Forge must be designed as a challenge platform.

Not as a one-off Exam Flame application.

All systems should assume:

Multiple Challenges

Multiple Seasons

Multiple Leaderboards

Multiple Hall Competitions

Thousands of Future Submissions

The architecture chosen today should remain valid even if Forge grows 100x larger than its initial launch.






CHATGPT ADMIN AND SUPER ADMIN ACHITECTURE:
# Administration & Governance Architecture

Forge is a competitive accountability platform where rankings determine status, prizes, and public recognition.

Because of this, trust is a foundational requirement.

The platform must be architected so that rankings are generated by the system, not manipulated by administrators.

Forge uses a three-role governance model:

- Participant
- Admin
- SuperAdmin

No additional roles should exist in Version 1.

The permission system should remain simple, understandable, and transparent.

---

## Governance Philosophy

Forge operates under a strict separation of responsibilities.

Participants compete.

Admins verify.

SuperAdmins govern.

This separation is critical.

Administrators should never have direct control over rankings, total hours, or leaderboard positions.

Rankings must always be generated automatically from verified submissions.

The platform must be designed so that trust comes from process rather than authority.

---

# Participant Role

Participants are competitors.

Their primary objective is to join challenges, submit progress, and climb leaderboards.

Participants can:

- Register an account
- Join active challenges
- Submit challenge entries
- Upload evidence
- View leaderboards
- View rankings
- View personal progress
- Edit their profile
- View their own submission history

Participants cannot:

- Review submissions
- Access moderation tools
- Modify challenge settings
- Access administrative areas
- View internal notes
- Change rankings

Participants should never see administrative interfaces.

The participant experience should remain focused entirely on competition and progress.

---

# Admin Role

Admins are operators.

They are not platform owners.

Their responsibility is to verify submissions, investigate suspicious activity, and maintain challenge integrity.

Admins function as referees.

They do not control challenges.

They do not control rankings.

They do not control platform configuration.

They simply ensure submitted data is legitimate.

---

## Admin Responsibilities

The primary responsibility of an Admin is submission verification.

Admins review participant submissions and determine whether they should contribute toward challenge rankings.

Each submission enters a moderation queue.

Submission states include:

- Pending
- Confirmed
- Rejected

Admins can:

- View pending submissions
- Inspect uploaded screenshots
- Review claimed hours
- Review topics studied
- Confirm submissions
- Reject submissions
- Add moderation notes
- Flag suspicious activity
- Suspend participants when necessary

Admins should not be capable of editing challenge settings or leaderboard calculations.

---

## Submission Review Workflow

The review process must be optimized for speed.

Admins should not navigate through complex dashboards.

Instead, Forge should provide a dedicated moderation queue.

When an Admin logs in, the first thing they should see is the review queue.

Example:

37 Pending Reviews

Selecting a submission should immediately display:

- Participant Name
- Claimed Hours
- Study Topic
- Screenshot Evidence
- Submission Timestamp
- Verification History

Available actions:

- Confirm
- Reject
- Skip

After a decision is made, the next submission should load automatically.

The verification experience should feel more like processing email than navigating dashboards.

Efficiency is the priority.

---

## Moderation Notes

Admins should be able to leave internal moderation notes.

Examples:

- Verified manually
- Duplicate screenshot detected
- User provided additional proof
- Suspicious timestamp
- Pending investigation

These notes must never be visible to participants.

They exist purely for administrative coordination.

---

## Participant Flags

Forge should support participant flagging.

Flags do not prevent participation.

Flags simply indicate that additional scrutiny may be required.

Examples:

- Repeated suspicious submissions
- Multiple rejected entries
- Reported by another participant

Flagged participants should be visually identified within the moderation interface.

---

## Participant Suspension

Admins may temporarily suspend a participant.

Suspended participants:

- Cannot submit entries
- Cannot join new challenges
- Remain visible on leaderboards
- Retain historical records

Suspensions should require a reason.

Every suspension must be logged.

---

# SuperAdmin Role

SuperAdmins govern the platform.

This role should be extremely limited.

In most deployments there should only be one or two SuperAdmins.

SuperAdmins possess all Admin permissions plus platform-level authority.

They are responsible for challenge creation, configuration, platform management, and governance.

---

## Challenge Creation

SuperAdmins can create challenges.

A challenge contains:

- Name
- Description
- Rules
- Duration
- Prize Structure
- Start Date
- End Date
- Status

Examples:

- Exam Flame
- ReadHabit
- CodeHabit
- Clash of Halls

The platform should never be hardcoded around a specific challenge.

---

## Challenge Lifecycle Management

SuperAdmins control challenge states.

Supported states include:

Draft

The challenge is being prepared.

Active

The challenge is live and accepting submissions.

Verification

Submissions are under review and final rankings are not yet locked.

Completed

Results are finalized.

Archived

Challenge history is preserved.

Only SuperAdmins can transition challenges between these states.

---

## User Management

SuperAdmins can:

- Promote Participants to Admins
- Demote Admins to Participants
- Ban Accounts
- Restore Accounts
- Reset Permissions

Role changes must always be logged.

---

## Challenge Configuration

SuperAdmins can modify:

- Prize structures
- Leaderboard rules
- Submission requirements
- Challenge duration
- Hall participation settings
- Verification requirements

Admins cannot access these controls.

---

# Leaderboard Integrity Rules

The leaderboard is the most important asset within Forge.

Because of this, strict integrity rules must exist.

Neither Admins nor SuperAdmins should ever directly edit:

- Leaderboard positions
- Total verified hours
- Ranking order
- Participant scores

These values must always be calculated automatically by the system.

Administrative users influence rankings only through submission verification.

No manual ranking controls should exist anywhere within the platform.

---

# Audit Log System

Forge must maintain a complete audit trail.

Every administrative action should generate an immutable audit record.

Examples:

Admin Sarah approved submission.

Admin David suspended participant.

SuperAdmin Prince modified challenge rules.

SuperAdmin Prince promoted user to Admin.

Audit logs should contain:

- User performing action
- Timestamp
- Action type
- Affected entity
- Previous state
- New state

Nothing should happen silently.

The platform should be fully traceable.

---

# Appeals System

Participants should be allowed to appeal rejected submissions.

An appeal contains:

- Submission Reference
- Participant Explanation
- Optional Additional Evidence

Appeals enter a dedicated review queue.

Administrators can:

- Uphold Rejection
- Restore Submission

Appeal outcomes should also be logged.

This creates transparency and reduces disputes.

---

# Fraud Detection Layer

Forge should automatically identify suspicious activity.

Examples include:

- Duplicate screenshots
- Excessive claimed hours
- Identical submissions across multiple days
- Unusual activity spikes
- Repeated rejected entries

The system should never automatically reject content.

Instead, suspicious submissions should be flagged for human review.

The administrator remains the final decision maker.

---

# Administrative Interface Philosophy

The administrative interface is not a dashboard.

It is an operations center.

Every design decision should prioritize:

- Speed
- Accuracy
- Visibility
- Efficiency

Administrators should spend as little time as possible navigating.

The system should bring work directly to them.

The goal is to make reviewing hundreds of submissions feel effortless.

---

# Governance Summary

Forge follows a strict separation of responsibilities:

Participants compete.

Admins verify.

SuperAdmins govern.

The system calculates rankings.

No human manually controls leaderboard positions.

Trust is achieved through transparency, auditability, and process integrity.

This governance structure should remain valid whether Forge supports 100 participants or 100,000 participants.




CHATGPT Authentication & Identity Architecture:
# Authentication & Identity Architecture

Forge is a competitive accountability platform.

Authentication exists to establish identity, accountability, and trust while introducing as little friction as possible.

The majority of Forge users will arrive from mobile devices through shared links distributed in WhatsApp groups.

Because of this, authentication should prioritize speed, simplicity, and accessibility over enterprise-level complexity.

The objective is simple:

A new participant should be able to discover Forge, create an account, join a challenge, and submit their first entry within approximately sixty seconds.

Authentication should feel invisible.

The user should think about competition, not account creation.

---

# Authentication Philosophy

Forge is not a banking application.

Forge is not an enterprise platform.

Forge does not require heavy onboarding.

The authentication experience should optimize for:

- Low friction
- Fast registration
- Mobile usability
- Persistent sessions
- Accountability
- Future scalability

The authentication system should avoid:

- Complex onboarding flows
- Mandatory password creation
- Password reset systems
- Multi-step verification processes
- Excessive form fields
- Administrative approval requirements

Every unnecessary screen increases abandonment.

The platform should remove as much friction as possible while maintaining trust and accountability.

---

# Supported Authentication Methods

Version 1 should support only two authentication providers.

Primary Provider:

Google Authentication

Secondary Provider:

Email One-Time Password (OTP)

No additional authentication providers should be implemented in Version 1.

Future providers may include:

- Apple
- Microsoft
- GitHub

However these should remain future considerations only.

---

# Google Authentication

Google should be the primary authentication method.

The majority of users already possess Google accounts.

Google sign-in provides the fastest onboarding experience.

The primary call-to-action on authentication screens should be:

Continue with Google

Google authentication should require a single tap whenever possible.

No additional registration flow should occur before account creation.

---

# Email OTP Authentication

Users who prefer not to use Google should be able to authenticate using email-based one-time passwords.

Workflow:

Enter Email

Receive Verification Code

Enter Verification Code

Account Created

No password should ever be required.

No password creation screen should exist.

No password recovery flow should exist.

The objective is to eliminate unnecessary complexity.

---

# Authentication Provider Architecture

Authentication should be powered by Supabase Auth.

Supabase should manage:

- User identity
- OAuth providers
- OTP delivery
- Session management
- Access tokens
- Refresh tokens

Forge should not implement custom authentication infrastructure.

Authentication should remain delegated to Supabase.

---

# Identity Creation Workflow

Authentication and profile creation are separate concepts.

Logging in creates an account.

Joining a challenge requires a complete profile.

After successful authentication, the user should be redirected to profile completion.

Required profile fields:

- Full Name
- Hall
- Course
- Academic Level

Optional fields:

- Profile Photo
- Phone Number

Users should not be allowed to join challenges until required profile information has been completed.

This ensures leaderboard entries remain meaningful and identifiable.

---

# User Identity Model

Each user should contain:

- Unique ID
- Full Name
- Email Address
- Profile Image
- Hall
- Course
- Academic Level
- Role
- Handle
- Created Date
- Last Login Date

The authentication provider should remain the source of truth for identity.

Forge-specific information should exist separately from authentication records.

---

# Forge Handle System

Each participant should possess a unique Forge handle.

Examples:

@prince

@goodness

@samuel

The handle should be unique across the platform.

Handles enable:

- Public profiles
- Future mentions
- Rival systems
- Community interactions

Leaderboards may display full names while handles remain available internally.

---

# Session Management

Forge is a mobile-first application.

Users should not be forced to repeatedly log in.

Sessions should remain persistent.

Recommended session behavior:

- 30-Day Session Duration
- Automatic Token Refresh
- Seamless Reauthentication

Participants should remain logged in throughout an entire challenge whenever possible.

The average user should not encounter a login screen twice during a single challenge.

---

# Mobile Authentication Experience

Authentication must be designed for mobile devices first.

Desktop experiences should adapt from the mobile design.

The authentication screen should remain extremely minimal.

Screen Structure:

Forge Wordmark

Short Value Statement

Primary Authentication Button

Divider

Secondary Authentication Button

Nothing else.

Avoid:

- Marketing sections
- Testimonials
- Feature grids
- Statistics
- Promotional content
- Complex navigation

The objective is account creation, not education.

---

# Challenge Join Architecture

Authentication does not automatically join a challenge.

A participant must explicitly join a challenge.

Workflow:

Authentication

Profile Completion

Challenge Page

Rule Acceptance

Challenge Enrollment

This separation allows one account to participate in multiple challenges.

---

# Rule Acceptance

Example:

I understand that submissions require evidence.

I understand that fraudulent submissions may result in disqualification.

I understand that rankings are determined by verified submissions.

---

# Invitation Link Architecture

Every challenge should possess a public invitation link.

Example:

forge.app/exam-flame

Authenticated Users:

Directly enter the challenge page.

Unauthenticated Users:

Authentication Flow

Automatic Return To Challenge

This behavior is critical for conversion and user retention.

# Role Assignment

Every newly created account should receive:

Participant

by default.

No exceptions.

Role elevation should only occur through administrative action.

Supported roles:

Participant

Admin

SuperAdmin

Users must never self-assign elevated permissions.

Client-side role validation alone is insufficient.

Participants:

Competition interfaces only.

- Password recovery flows

- Support requests

- Refresh token rotation
- Protected API routes

- Audit logging for sensitive actions

---

# Authentication Design Principles

Authentication should feel effortless.

Users should never feel like they are creating an account.

Users should feel like they are entering a competition.

The entire experience should communicate:

Quick Entry

Low Friction

Strong Identity

High Accountability

Every design decision should support these goals.

Sessions should remain persistent.

Recommended session behavior:

- 30-Day Session Duration
- Automatic Token Refresh
- Seamless Reauthentication

Participants should remain logged in throughout an entire challenge whenever possible.

The average user should not encounter a login screen twice during a single challenge.

---

# Mobile Authentication Experience

Authentication must be designed for mobile devices first.

Desktop experiences should adapt from the mobile design.

The authentication screen should remain extremely minimal.

Screen Structure:

Forge Wordmark

Short Value Statement

Primary Authentication Button

Divider

Secondary Authentication Button

Nothing else.

Avoid:

- Marketing sections
- Testimonials
- Feature grids
- Statistics
- Promotional content
- Complex navigation

The objective is account creation, not education.

---

# Challenge Join Architecture

Authentication does not automatically join a challenge.

A participant must explicitly join a challenge.

Workflow:

Authentication

Profile Completion

Challenge Page

Rule Acceptance

Challenge Enrollment

This separation allows one account to participate in multiple challenges.

---

# Rule Acceptance

Before joining a challenge, participants should acknowledge challenge rules.

Example:

I understand that submissions require evidence.

I understand that fraudulent submissions may result in disqualification.

I understand that rankings are determined by verified submissions.

Participants must actively confirm these acknowledgements before joining.

This creates accountability and reduces disputes.

---

# Invitation Link Architecture

Every challenge should possess a public invitation link.

Example:

forge.app/exam-flame

Users accessing challenge links should experience intelligent routing.

Authenticated Users:

Directly enter the challenge page.

Unauthenticated Users:

Authentication Flow

Automatic Return To Challenge

The platform should always return users to their intended destination after login.

This behavior is critical for conversion and user retention.

---

# Role Assignment

Every newly created account should receive:

Participant

by default.

No exceptions.

Role elevation should only occur through administrative action.

Supported roles:

Participant

Admin

SuperAdmin

Users must never self-assign elevated permissions.

---

# Role-Based Access Control

Access should be determined server-side.

Client-side role validation alone is insufficient.

Protected routes should verify permissions before rendering content.

Participants:

Competition interfaces only.

Admins:

Verification and moderation interfaces.

SuperAdmins:

Governance and platform management interfaces.

Authentication should not determine experience.

Role assignment should determine experience.

---

# Account Recovery

Because Forge uses Google Authentication and Email OTP Authentication, traditional password recovery should not exist.

Google users recover accounts through Google.

Email OTP users recover access through email verification.

This removes the need for:

- Password reset screens
- Password recovery flows
- Security questions
- Support requests

The authentication system should remain maintenance-free.

---

# Security Requirements

Minimum security requirements include:

- Secure session storage
- Refresh token rotation
- Protected API routes
- Server-side permission validation
- Row-Level Security
- Secure cookie handling
- Audit logging for sensitive actions

Client-side permissions should never be trusted.

The server remains the ultimate authority for access control.

---

# Authentication Analytics

Forge should collect authentication analytics.

Examples:

- New Registrations
- Authentication Method Usage
- Challenge Join Conversion Rate
- Registration Completion Rate
- Abandoned Profile Completion

These metrics should be available to SuperAdmins.

The objective is continuous optimization of onboarding.

---

# Authentication Design Principles

Authentication should feel effortless.

Users should never feel like they are creating an account.

Users should feel like they are entering a competition.

The entire experience should communicate:

Quick Entry

Low Friction

Strong Identity

High Accountability

Every design decision should support these goals.

Success is achieved when a participant can move from a WhatsApp link to active challenge participation in under one minute.
